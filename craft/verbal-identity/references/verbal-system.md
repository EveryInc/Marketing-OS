# Verbal System

## Voice versus tone

Voice is the stable relationship and point of view. Tone changes with audience, stakes,
channel, and moment.

Write each trait as a behavior:

| Weak | Useful |
|---|---|
| Smart | Names the useful distinction and shows the evidence |
| Friendly | Assumes intelligence and explains without making the reader feel behind |
| Bold | Makes a defensible claim and accepts the tradeoff |
| Playful | Uses wit to reveal something, never to avoid substance |

## Required system

### Voice in one line

Define the role the brand plays and the manner in which it plays it.

### Default behavior

Specify how openings work, how authority is earned, how evidence appears, how abstraction
becomes concrete, and what the ending asks the reader to do or reconsider.

### Tone modes

Use three or four named modes. State the default and the surfaces for each. Limit each
output to a primary mode and one secondary mode.

### Surface registers

Define how the same voice behaves in editorial, product, sales, support, events, social,
and internal communication. Give each a governing job or verb.

### Language codes

Include vocabulary, cadence, syntax, punctuation, naming conventions, claims discipline,
and prohibited shortcuts. Preserve real edge and idiosyncrasy; do not reduce the system to
grammar rules.

### Examples

Use paired examples to reveal the rule. Include enough context to explain why one line
belongs and the other does not. Avoid examples so polished that writers copy them as
templates.

## Evaluation

A strong system lets different writers produce work that belongs to the same brand. It
also rejects fluent copy that violates the brand's philosophy, customer relationship, or
position.
