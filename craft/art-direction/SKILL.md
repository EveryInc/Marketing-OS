# Art Direction

Build a defensible visual identity from approved strategy, character, verbal identity,
competitive context, semiotics, and human-curated references. The output is a visual
system capable of producing real surfaces, not a moodboard or a pile of generated assets.

## When to invoke

- When the compound-brand workflow reaches visual identity
- When creating or rebuilding a digital product, event, or master-brand identity
- When a team needs visual territories, competitive differentiation, or reference guidance
- When a moodboard feels attractive but arbitrary, generic, or hard to extend
- When evaluating whether AI-generated imagery belongs in a designed brand world

## Preconditions

Require approved strategic foundation, brand character, and verbal identity. Require the
product and channel context: what it does, where it appears, how people encounter it, and
what physical, digital, print, OOH, motion, environmental, and merchandise applications
matter.

Require five to 10 human-supplied references and at least three anti-references. Use
`references/reference-doctrine.md` to guide the human. Do not proceed from generic labels
such as Bauhaus, vintage editorial, graffiti, or surrealism.

## Audit

Load `references/competitive-semiotic-audit.md`. Audit competitors' marks, typography,
color, composition, imagery, motion, materials, interfaces, and category codes. Return a
fit-in versus stand-out diagnosis. Recommend standing out unless category legibility or an
intentional dupe strategy requires borrowed codes.

## Territories

Offer no more than two:

1. Functional-led: organized around what the brand demonstrates or helps people do
2. Emotional-led: organized around the feeling, identity, or cultural tension it owns

Each territory must combine at least two specific reference domains in a way that feels
new in the category. Explain the conceptual bridge. Present governing idea, reference
logic, semiotic codes, system grammar, risks, anti-references, and surface applications.

The human chooses or edits a territory before asset development.

## Visual identity system

Load `references/visual-identity-system.md`. Define:

- Brandmark and wordmark
- Type hierarchy
- Color system
- Layout and compositional grammar
- Image, illustration, and photography direction
- Glyphs, sigils, or icons when needed
- Texture, material, and brand-world elements
- Motion behavior
- Digital product and website expression
- Print, OOH, merchandise, environmental, and accessibility behavior

Describe how the system behaves before discussing minute applications. A badge, headshot
treatment, or token can prove the system later; it should not substitute for the system.

## Asset generation

Art direction may specify commissioned photography, illustration, design, or controlled
AI generation. Do not assume the AI will create final assets. Name the production method,
tool, human role, iteration loop, and quality-control standard. Generated imagery must
share recurring rules and survive human art direction and postproduction.

## Quick checklist

- [ ] Strategy, character, and verbal identity were approved first
- [ ] Product, channel, and production context are explicit
- [ ] Human supplied five to 10 strong references and three anti-references
- [ ] Competitive visual and semiotic audit is complete
- [ ] References are specific, category-fresh, and system-generating
- [ ] One functional-led and one emotional-led territory were presented
- [ ] The human locked the territory before asset development
- [ ] Every core visual identity component is defined
- [ ] System behavior works across digital, print, OOH, motion, and merchandise
- [ ] Any generated asset has a named human art-direction and postproduction loop
