# Competitive and Semiotic Audit

## Audit the field

Capture direct competitors, substitutes, adjacent categories, and category leaders.
For each, record:

- Mark and naming behavior
- Type categories and hierarchy
- Dominant and secondary colors
- Composition and information density
- Photography, illustration, 3D, and generative imagery
- Motion and interaction
- Materials, packaging, and physical presence
- Repeated symbols and metaphors
- Claimed personality and actual felt personality
- Distinctive assets versus category wallpaper

## Read the codes

Use semiotics to identify what the visual choices signify in the customer's context.
A red cola can borrow Coke's shelf equity. A black-and-neon AI event can disappear into
the category even when it looks polished. Meaning depends on product, culture, channel,
and place of encounter.

Separate:

- **Icon:** resembles the thing
- **Index:** points to evidence or effect
- **Symbol:** carries learned cultural meaning
- **Category code:** helps the audience recognize what kind of thing this is
- **Distinctive asset:** repeatedly and uniquely identifies this brand

## Fit in versus stand out

Return two lists:

- Codes the brand must retain for category legibility
- Codes the brand should reject, invert, or replace for distinction

Recommend standing out by default. Fit in when unfamiliarity would block comprehension,
trust, safety, or distribution. Intentional dupe products may borrow more category equity,
but the choice must be explicit.

## Output

- Competitor table
- Category-code inventory
- Semiotic opportunity map
- Fit-in versus stand-out recommendation
- Visual white space
- Recuperation risks
- Implications for each proposed territory
