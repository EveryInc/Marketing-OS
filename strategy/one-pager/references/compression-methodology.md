# Compression Methodology

Compression removes redundancy and lowers reading time without flattening the strategy.

## Protect before cutting

Create an internal preservation list before editing:

- Core argument or decision.
- Distinctive creative premise.
- Target audience and desired movement.
- Operating mechanism.
- Subscriber, Consulting, fame, or authority path.
- Essential evidence or intellectual tension.
- Owner, dependencies, cost, and decision gate.

If a cut removes a unique item from this list, reverse it.

## What to cut

- Repeated rationale.
- Theory that does not change a decision.
- Process history.
- Multiple examples proving the same point.
- Caveats that belong in the full strategy.
- Low-value adjectives and consultant language.

## Process

1. Read the full source and identify its argument.
2. Separate unique information from repetition.
3. Choose the format and audience.
4. Draft the decision, rationale, program, measurement, asks, and budget in that order
   when relevant.
5. Compare the compressed version against the preservation list.
6. Keep the full strategy linked and intact.

## Failure test

The compression failed if:

- The idea now sounds interchangeable with a competitor's.
- The reader understands what the program is called but not how it works.
- Measurement survives but the creative premise disappears.
- The executive page contradicts or silently replaces the source.
- A team could approve the page but could not execute the work.
