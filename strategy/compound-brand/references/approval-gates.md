# Approval Gates

## Interaction contract

Ask one consequential question at a time. Use the answer to narrow the next question.
Avoid questionnaires that ask the human to solve the strategy in advance.

For every major decision:

1. State the evidence and the implication.
2. Present two or three developed options.
3. Recommend one and explain the tradeoff.
4. Ask the human to approve, choose, edit, or reject.
5. Record the ruling before continuing.

## Required gates

### Gate one: research sufficiency

Confirm the corpus is adequate for a first recommendation. Mark every important claim as
verified, directional, qualitative, inferred, or untested.

### Gate two: customer and demand

Lock the primary customer, their stakes, and the demand the brand will cultivate.

### Gate three: competitive map

Show four or five axis pairs. Require a human choice or edit before locking the matrix.

### Gate four: strategic foundation

Present the complete top half of the brand book. Do not proceed to character until the
human approves the foundation.

### Gate five: character

Approve archetype, personality, behavioral traits, relationship, and anti-traits.

### Gate six: equity depth

Explain the Aaker-led equity phase. The human chooses `complete now` or `defer`. A deferral
must include a return trigger.

### Gate seven: verbal territory

Present one functional-led and one emotional-led territory. Lock one direction or record
the precise synthesis.

### Gate eight: references

Require five to 10 human-supplied references and at least three anti-references. Reject
generic labels such as “graffiti” or “vintage” until the human makes them specific.

### Gate nine: visual territory

Present one functional-led and one emotional-led territory. Lock the governing idea,
reference logic, and system principles before asset development.

## New-brand fallback

When the brand has no customers, allow the first-party survey step to be skipped. Build a
public research corpus and label the customer model as provisional. Add post-launch
triggers rather than designing an elaborate validation program prematurely:

- Revisit after the first meaningful customer cohort
- Survey when a sample can change a decision
- Interview retained, high-usage, and churned customers
- Re-run the competitive audit when the category changes materially

## Decision log

Record date, decision, options considered, evidence, owner, ruling, and revisit trigger.
This turns later revisions into compound learning rather than a new process from zero.
