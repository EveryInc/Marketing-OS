# Strategic Foundation

## Purpose

Produce the complete, reviewable top half of a brand book. The work should replace a
two- or three-week first research and synthesis pass, while keeping the human responsible
for final strategic judgment.

## Hierarchy of decisions

1. **Enduring philosophy:** what the business believes, why it exists, and the future it
   is trying to make. A sound philosophy should not reverse with a campaign or customer
   trend.
2. **Customer:** who needs the solution, what they are trying to become, and what evidence
   supports the choice.
3. **Demand:** the problem, desire, tension, or latent need the brand can make salient.
   Marketing can teach a customer to value an overlooked benefit when the product truly
   delivers it.
4. **Mental position:** the association the brand intends to own in the customer's mind,
   following Ries and Trout. Market position and mental position may differ.
5. **Expression:** positioning statement, value propositions, proof, messaging, character,
   voice, and design.

Customer demand and the ownable position should reinforce each other. Competitive brands
may address the same demand through different frames. The chosen frame must be credible,
distinctive, and useful to the business.

## Required inputs

- Business model, product, price, distribution, and growth ambition
- Founder or leadership interviews
- Existing purpose, mission, vision, values, and non-negotiable beliefs
- Product demonstrations and actual customer experience
- Customer data, surveys, interviews, reviews, support logs, churn reasons, and usage data
- Competitor list, adjacent categories, substitutes, and cultural alternatives
- Category history and current language
- Existing brand assets, campaigns, and performance evidence

When first-party evidence is unavailable, create an online corpus using recent named
sources: category studies, credible surveys, ethnography, interviews, review sites,
forums, social posts, memes, search behavior, competitor material, and public datasets.
Separate observed evidence from inference.

## Analysis

### Customer and demand

Define the primary customer with behavior and stakes, not demographics alone. Capture:

- Current behavior and workarounds
- Functional job and desired progress
- Emotional and social stakes
- Trigger for action
- Objections and switching costs
- Language used without prompting
- Evidence strength and missing validation

### Competitive audit

Audit what competitors claim, prove, look like, sound like, sell, and repeatedly own.
Include direct competitors, substitutes, adjacent categories, and the default behavior of
doing nothing.

The positioning matrix is required. Generate four or five candidate axis pairs from the
evidence. Each pair must be meaningful to customer choice, independent enough to reveal
real separation, and capable of placing every major competitor without distortion. Ask
the human to choose or edit the axes before drawing conclusions.

### Positioning options

Offer two or three coherent positions. For each, state:

- Mental association to own
- Target customer and demand
- Competitive frame
- Reason to believe
- Business advantage
- Risk, tradeoff, and what the brand must refuse

Recommend one. Do not merge incompatible positions into a consensus statement.

## Top-half output

1. Purpose
2. Mission
3. Vision
4. Values and operating philosophy
5. Category and competitive frame
6. Primary and secondary audiences
7. Demand model
8. Positioning matrix
9. Mental position
10. Positioning statement
11. Value propositions and proof
12. Brand essence or enduring promise
13. Decision log and validation plan

Generate two or three options for positioning statements and other compact strategic
language. Require approval, edits, or explicit notes before lock.
