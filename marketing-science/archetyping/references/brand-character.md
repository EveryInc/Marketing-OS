# Brand Character

## Build from strategy

Start with the brand's enduring philosophy, the customer relationship, and the behavior
required to deliver the position. Ask:

- What role should the brand play in the customer's life?
- What does the brand help the customer become?
- What behavior makes the philosophy visible?
- What tension must the character hold?
- How would the brand act when challenged, wrong, successful, or under pressure?
- Which behavior would betray the brand even if it performed well?

## Model

### Archetypal pattern

Use one primary pattern and, when needed, one secondary pattern. Explain the combination in
plain language. Avoid selecting an archetype because its visual symbols are attractive.

### Relationship

Name the brand's role: guide, peer, challenger, host, craftsperson, advocate, teacher,
co-conspirator, institution, or another precise relationship. State the power dynamic and
what the brand never does to the customer.

### Behavioral traits

For each trait, provide:

- What the trait means
- Observable behavior
- What it rejects
- A high-stakes example
- A likely failure mode

### Shadow

Every strength can reverse. Authority becomes dogma. Play becomes frivolity. Care becomes
control. Rebellion becomes attention seeking. Name the shadow and the editorial or design
gate that prevents it.

### Range

Show character across product, editorial, sales, support, events, and internal culture.
The manner changes with context; the philosophy and relationship remain.

## Output

- Character in one line
- Primary and secondary archetype
- Role and relationship
- Core desire and fear
- Traits and anti-traits
- Shadow risks
- Channel range
- Implications for equity, verbal identity, and art direction
