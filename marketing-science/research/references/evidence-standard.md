# Evidence Standard

## Source order

Prefer evidence in this order when it is available and relevant:

1. Product behavior, transactions, retention, churn, and direct customer records
2. Proprietary surveys and interviews with the intended customer
3. Recent research with a sample and method appropriate to the claim
4. Ethnography, field observation, support conversations, and sales calls
5. Reviews, forums, search behavior, memes, and public internet chatter
6. Competitor claims and category commentary
7. Expert judgment and inference

Lower-ranked evidence can reveal language and emerging demand that a survey misses. It
must not be presented as statistically representative.

## Recency

Use the most recent defensible source. For changing categories, prefer the last one or two
years. Older sources can establish enduring behavior, category history, or a theoretical
base. State why an older source still applies.

## Sample and method

Record sample size, respondent definition, geography, field date, recruitment, question
wording, and known bias when available. “Statistically significant” is meaningful only
relative to the population, design, and claim.

For directional customer surveys, several hundred qualified responses can be strong.
For qualitative work, depth and purposeful sampling matter more than count. Twenty well-
chosen superuser interviews may explain mechanisms that a broad survey only measures.

## Confidence labels

- **Verified:** direct, current evidence supports the claim
- **Strong directional:** several credible sources agree, with stated limitations
- **Qualitative pattern:** repeated in interviews or observed behavior
- **Cultural signal:** visible in language, memes, or community behavior
- **Inference:** analyst interpretation connecting evidence
- **Hypothesis:** plausible and important, awaiting validation

## Evidence ledger

Use these fields:

| ID | Claim | Source | Date | Sample/method | Audience | Confidence | Limitation | Strategic use | Recheck |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Every strategic recommendation should cite ledger IDs. Never invent precision to make a
recommendation feel settled.
