## Effectiveness stance

Every operates in a category that rewards cultural gravity, not reach optimization. Our effectiveness model rests on five claims.

1. **Brand building and growth marketing are separate disciplines.** Marketing owns brand equity, cultural authority, and awareness among audiences who aren't in-market yet. Growth owns conversion, funnel mechanics, and activation among audiences who are. Confusing them produces work that does neither job well. The classical 60/40 framing isn't a budget rule for us — we barely spend on paid — it's a mandate split. Marketing's output is the soil. Growth's output is the harvest. Growth converts demand. Marketing creates it.

2. **We measure what matters culturally, not what's easiest to attribute.** North-star marketing metrics: earned press placements in taste-making outlets (not tech trade press), organic newsletter subscribers, and qualitative brand perception among creative and cultural professionals. We do not measure click-through, CAC, or MQLs. Those belong to Growth. Our job is to make them cheaper to acquire.

3. **The badge brand thesis is Every's central organizing principle.** "I read Every" must carry the same weight as "I read The Economist." Subscribing — especially at the Max tier — is a statement of identity. This is an uncrossable moat if we execute it.

   **The luxury software principle:** You can get very good software for free. So subscribing to Every needs to mean something irrational, emotional — a badge brand, a luxury item. The experience must be elevated, full of surprise and delight, and stunningly gorgeous. Reading the newsletter should feel like receiving secret knowledge.

   **Market moment (Moore's Chasm):** AI is crossing the chasm. The innovators are in. The early majority is arriving now — motivated not by enthusiasm but by professional survival. From "F*ck AI" to "F*ck, I have to figure out AI." Key data: 72% of knowledge workers using AI (BCG 2025), 900M ChatGPT weekly users (OpenAI/TechCrunch 2026), only 1% of companies AI-mature (McKinsey 2025), +315% AI&I audience YoY (Every internal).

   **Through-line archetype across all audiences:** High agency. Professionally ambitious. Taste-conscious. Anxious to move fast. Intellectually honest about what they don't know. The badge matters — not as vanity, but as identity.

   **Four target audiences (canonized May 2026):**
   - **T1 Media** — AI-Informed Professional, 28–45, mid-career. Wants signal over noise. The gateway to Every. Reads NYT, Stratechery, The Verge. Competes with Morning Brew AI, TLDR, The Neuron. Every wins on depth + taste.
   - **T2 Studio/Bundle** — AI-Powered Operator, 30–45, $20–35/mo. Where the badge thesis lives or dies. Browses ProductHunt, reads Lenny's Newsletter. Competing with standalone tools (Notion AI, Jasper, Otter). Every wins because the suite is curated by people who write about AI daily.
   - **T3 Consulting** — Enterprise Buyer, 40–55, C-suite. $100K–$1M+ engagements. Reads Bloomberg, HBR, listens to Acquired. Competes with McKinsey Digital, BCG, Accenture. Every wins on practitioner credibility — we build, not just advise.
   - **T4 Education** — Motivated Upskiller, 25–40. Career-anxious. Structured learning, not YouTube. Reads Reforge, Maven, follows Sahil Lavingia. Competes with Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Reforge. Every wins on practitioner depth + editorial authority.

   **Niche vs. broad?** Stay specific, let aspiration do the broadening. The Economist's circulation is 1.2M — its cultural footprint is 100× that.

   The comp set for master brand voice and cultural positioning: A24, Monocle, Teenage Engineering, Nothing, Claude/Anthropic, Perplexity, Vintage IBM, Apple, Ralph Lauren, The Economist, Bauhaus/Black Mountain College, Chanel, Bottega Veneta, Loewe, Bloomberg, Pentagram, Collins, Browser Co. of NY, Buly 1803, Fornasetti, Hendrick's Gin, Stripe Press, Penguin Publishing. Structural models: Penguin (color as information architecture, design as catalog-level asset — "the goal is a shelf") and IBM (design making complex technology feel coherent).

4. **Creative quality is the effectiveness lever, because we can't out-spend the AI field.** OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google can outspend any marketing budget we'd ever have. We can't win on reach bought. We can win on idea density, cultural taste, and craft. Nike in the 80s and 90s and Apple in the early 2000s are the canonical examples of how to build enormous brand value without CPG-scale budgets — by making every piece of output worth paying attention to. Our native surfaces are different (essays, software UI, podcasts, conferences, occasional campaigns), but the principle transfers: the work itself is the marketing.

5. **Branded house architecture (decided May 2026).** Every is a branded house, not a house of brands. Like Google, Nike, Ralph Lauren, Apple, Adobe. Architecture rules:
   - Master brand is always visible
   - Products are siblings, not strangers (Spiral, Cora, Sparkle, Monologue, Proof, Plus One)
   - Consulting is "the twin who went to business school" — same DNA, different dialect
   - Editorial is the brand's front door
   - Events and education inherit from the master brand and adapt

   The newsletter builds cultural authority that sells software. The software generates use cases and case studies that fuel the newsletter. Writers and operators become cultural figures who amplify the whole. No sub-brand is marketed on feature parity with a category incumbent. Every sub-brand is marketed as an object from the studio.

   **Emotional register (canonized May 2026):**
   - NOT: Loud, minimalist-tech-bro, literary-precious, American dynamism, political
   - IS: Confident and warm, editorial and unexpected, intellectually ambitious, culturally aware, curious and profound
   - "The kind of brand that makes you feel smarter for knowing about it, because it actually does make you smarter."

   **Anti-patterns (if it could be swapped onto a competitor without anyone noticing, it's wrong):**
   Sepia intelligence · Notebook nostalgia · Literary preciousness · Quiet-luxury Substack-ism · "Serious writer" cosplay · Generic AI aesthetic · Category wallpaper · Corporate SaaS blandness

## Creative doctrine

What makes a great Every ad, email, launch line, or piece of copy great is the same thing that makes any ad great: you remember it. You remember the product, the tagline, but most importantly you remember what the brand does and how it made you feel. Google's Parisian Love. Jordan in the 1980s. Patagonia "Don't Buy This Jacket." The best ones stand out by being disruptive or counterintuitive or visually differentiated — and they exploit a unique tension or tell a story that makes you see how the product fits your world. They make you covet it. They make you feel something. The best ads are usually divisive at launch. They know who their customer is. They don't care about alienating people who aren't.

For Every, this means doing things no one else could do in the sea of AI sameness. We're the intellectual leader. The tastemaker. Apple to everyone else's PC. Hermès to LVMH. FP Journe to Rolex. We win on emotion, on badge brand status, and on identity-driving campaigns people share.

**Tonal range.** The creative can and should move at the pace of culture, but always retain tonal consistency. Think Benetton or Apple. We can be dry, deadpan, charming, whimsical — Wes Anderson at his best. Not absurd for its own sake.

**Smart vs. clever.** Smart is clever. Too-clever is obvious or self-congratulatory — so then it's not really clever.

**An Every line must never:** Pander or promote panic. Say something the reader already knows about AI.

**When minimalism becomes bloodless:** Most of the time. Bauhaus is played out. No one will ever be Apple again. Apple is already Apple.

**When maximal craft becomes try-hard:** When it doesn't have a story or thought-out system behind it.

**Canonical creative references beyond the comp set:** Not other brands — aesthetic and artistic movements. Bauhaus, Black Mountain College, the Stoics, the Decadents, Italian Futurism, Viennese Expressionism, Fauvism, Warhol's Factory, hip-hop, jazz. The codes of culture are what we use to build the culture for a new world of AI that currently doesn't exist. Taste is missing. But it's a buzzword now. Show, don't tell.

## Brand vs. performance

Marketing creates demand. Growth converts it. Our job is marketing, not growth.

CAC, MQLs, click-through — those are Austin's territory, the growth team's. They are not rejected; they're important. But the marketing OS exists to drive awareness, generate attention and buzz, build distinctive brand assets, and make people join the cult of Every.

**Allocation.** 80:20 brand to performance. Even with significant funding, paid would likely go to events, collaborations, and creating things — not ads. We may run OOH occasionally.

**Performance thinking does not influence marketing.** It belongs to the growth team. Our focus is brand salience and cohesion.

**A brand-building success in 2026 that wouldn't show up in attribution:** A collaboration with a brand like Teenage Engineering. A viral launch.

**A fake brand win:** Echo chamber — making us even more popular among AI tech nerds who already love us. The goal is to make their boss love us. And their girlfriend. And their boss's girlfriend.

## Taste as strategic asset

**Taste defined.** Taste is point of view — the judgment that something belongs. Which idea is worth writing about, which AI tool is worth covering, which guest is worth interviewing, which sentence earns its place, which campaign is worth shipping. Taste is the quality of attention paid to a decision most companies make on autopilot. A high taste level means, by definition, that some people won't "get it." Good.

Taste is largely philosophical, and we have a strong, clear philosophy rooted in Dan Shipper's work.

**What Every recognizes first.** The signal under the hype cycle. We are tastemakers because we live in the future and report back. People love our Vibe Checks — they want us to review AI models and workflows the way a critic would a fashion show in the 90s or a film in the Criterion Closet. The cultural shift inside AI that the rest of media writes about six months later starts with Every. If you follow us, you can surf the models with us, learn to work with AI better than everyone else, and articulate the wonder of AI without being a doomer or a utopian.

**What Every refuses even when the market rewards it.** Engagement bait. Generic AI-newsletter format. Naming convention slop ("X 4.0 Changes Everything"). The "10 prompts that will make you rich" register. Performance creative dressed as brand creative. Influencer programs that flatten the publication's voice. Sponsored content that doesn't sound like Every. Pretending to have an opinion on a topic the team hasn't actually worked on.

**The judgment hierarchy.** Editorial first — does the idea hold up. Strategic second — does it advance the brand. Aesthetic third — does it look the way Every looks. Moral fourth — would the team be proud of this in five years. When they conflict, the higher rank wins.

**"This looks like shit" triggers.** Gradients. Cartoonish robot mascots. "AI-powered" badges. Stock photography of people staring at laptops. Three-feature pricing tables. Headlines that start with "Revolutionary." Manifestos with no specifics. Founder pictures with arms crossed. Pastel purple applied to AI products. Drop-shadow chrome. Wordmarks with connector dots. Abstract Lottie animations. Hero copy that ends in a question mark. The word "supercharged."

**Taste vs. preference.** Taste survives outside the room. If three people who don't know each other independently pick the same option for related reasons, that's taste. If one person likes it and can't say why, that's preference. Preference is a fine starting point. Taste is what's left after pressure-testing.

**Populism vs. friction.** The work should be legible to an ambitious 28-year-old PM at a SaaS company who's never read Every before, and it should reward the reader who keeps coming back. Friction is fine when it earns the second read. Friction for its own sake is bad writing. We maintain our highly technical audience while growing to the AI-curious just beginning their journey. Customers describe Every as a "filter" — above all we are trusted because we are human beings talking about AI in a very human way.

**The "too obvious" failure mode.** Sounding like a vendor. Three benefit bullets with sans-serif icons. "Empowering teams to do their best work." Anything that could appear on any AI company's landing page without changing a word. Promoting content from models without disclosing it. Ads that don't make sense for us — think about how The Paris Review is selective about their advertisers, or how Monocle designs most of the ads in their pages. That's our bar.

## AI-native adaptations

AI is an instrument we play, not an efficiency tool we deploy.

**The fundamental advantage: building the thing while writing about it.** Most marketing is a department writing copy about a product they didn't make and barely use. Every flips that. The same people writing about AI are the people building with AI, and the writing is sharper because of it. The marketing isn't a translation of the work. It's the same act, made public. Dan Shipper calls this "creative alchemy."

**The real advantage isn't speed, personalization, or volume.** Those are the answers a SaaS company gives. The actual advantage is permission to play. Every can build a tool over a weekend because somebody on the team wants it, ship it to 50 readers, see what happens, kill it if nothing happens, keep going if something does. Monologue, Spiral, Cora, Sparkle — every product started from a personal itch, not a market-sizing exercise. The cost of going from "I want this" to a working thing in the world has collapsed. The advantage is being willing to use that.

**What we never automate.** The decision to make the thing. The opinion. The willingness to be wrong in public. The first sentence of an essay. The launch line. The headline. The choice of who to interview and why. The risk. Anything that, if it works, becomes part of why Every is Every.

**What we aggressively automate.** Everything between the idea and the world. Format adaptation across surfaces. Voice enforcement — Spiral is literally this. Variant generation under a sharp brief. The unglamorous middle 80% of any production process. The rule: a human had a thought worth having; the instrument extends that thought into more shapes without diluting it. We are the bread in the AI sandwich.

**How AI changes campaign strategy.** It collapses the distance between a hunch and a finished piece. Old strategy: imagine, brief, produce, ship, measure. New strategy: ship a version Wednesday, watch what happens, ship the better version Friday, keep what worked, discard what didn't. Strategy becomes a continuous experiment instead of an annual plan. The campaign is alive while it's running, not finished when it launches.

**What an AI-native launch looks like.** It has play in it. At least one piece of the launch is something only an AI-native team could've made — a small custom agent, an interactive site, a CLI, a generative tool — that the audience can actually touch rather than read about. AI Diplomacy is the model: an Every experiment that turned into 60,000 readers, 50,000 Twitch viewers, and a spinout company because the team thought it'd be fun to make frontier models play a board game against each other. Launches teach the team something a focus group never could because they let people surprise you.

**The products are the OS, not proof of it.** Spiral is how marketing copy gets written in the brand voice. Cora keeps email from eating the workday. Plus One handles the connective tissue of running a small ambitious team. Sparkle organizes files by what's inside them. Monologue captures thinking as it happens. The publication is the room where the thinking lands and the audience that closes the loop. Each tool earns its place by making the next piece of work better than the last. Dan's term: "artisanal AI apps." Small-batch software with a point of view, made by the people who use it.

**Compound marketing.** Every piece of marketing leaves something behind that the next piece inherits — a line, a format, a visual move, a tool the team built, a relationship with a reader who shows up again. Nothing starts from zero. Nothing dies after one cycle. The brand is what's left after years of stacking. The compounding is the strategy.

**Audience research.** Skip most of it. The audience is in the inbox, the comments, the podcast replies, the Discord. Reading them in real time, in their own words, with an instrument that lets a small team keep up with the volume, is worth more than any survey designed to extract a confirmation. The research is the conversation, ongoing.

**Relationships without being creepy.** Pay attention. Most companies don't actually read what the people in their orbit are saying. The right use of AI is to remember more of it, more carefully, and use that memory to write a better email, a sharper pitch, a reply that proves someone was actually listening. The rule is the same as before AI existed — the relationship is real or it isn't. AI just removes the excuse that there wasn't time.

## The Every difference

**Every is not a media company** because the publication is the spine, not the product. A media company sells attention to advertisers. Every sells editorial judgment, software people pay for monthly, and membership in something. The publication is what's underneath. The software is the body. The brand is the room people want to be in.

**We are a third thing.** More of a creative studio that ships software, writes essays, hosts a podcast, runs a paid community, and treats all of it as one body of work made by the same people for the same reasons. Closest analogues: A24 (curator with its own product line), Teenage Engineering (small group with strong taste shipping objects), and Stripe (operating company with serious editorial gravity). None is a media company. None is just a tech company.

### What The New Yorker gets right and wrong

**Right:** An editorial voice can compound for a century. Named writers as the masthead. Long-form as a form, not a length. Recurring rituals — the cartoons, the crossword, Talk of the Town, the Festival, the covers — as distinctive brand assets that compound across decades. Subscriber-funded resilience. The fact-checking apparatus as a signal of seriousness. Every should steal the masthead discipline, the recurring rituals (AI & I podcast, the annual essay, Chain of Thought, the model-of-the-year), and the editorial standard.

**Wrong for Every:** The New Yorker writes from a posture of skepticism toward technology and AI. Their voice treats new things as suspicious until proven otherwise. Every is the opposite — inside the thing, building with it, reporting from within. The New Yorker also can't ship software, can't release a tool on a Wednesday. They're a magazine that ports to the internet, not a studio that lives there. The cadence is wrong. The skepticism is wrong. The institutional ownership is the wrong shape. Borrow the masthead-and-ritual layer. Ignore the rest.

### What A24 gets right and wrong

**Right:** Master-brand discipline. The logo on the poster is a reason to show up. Every film, every piece of merch, every zine reinforces "A24" as the source of meaning. They moved from distribution to a $3.5B master brand by treating taste as the moat and merch as a portable billboard. AAA24 membership: quarterly zines, welcome packs, birthday gifts, early-access drops. Stunts over banner ads. They give artists permission to make weird, specific things and sell that permission as a brand. Every should steal the master-brand discipline, the membership infrastructure, the stunts philosophy, and the merch-as-marketing-channel logic.

**Wrong for Every:** A24 curates other people's work. We make our own. Their surface codes (Gen Z, indie horror, lo-fi nostalgia) aren't ours — take the operating posture, not the aesthetic. A24 optimizes for cultural cachet; Every needs to optimize for creative leverage in people's lives — you can write better with Spiral, but you can't directly use a Daniels movie. And A24 can't ship software, can't compound its brand into operating tools. Same shape until the audience needs an instrument; A24 stops there, we start there.

### Positioning clarity

**What Every understands about AI that tech media misses.** AI is a creative medium, not a category to cover. Tech media writes about AI like chips — vendor news, benchmarks, funding rounds. Every writes about AI like a magazine writes about an art form: what's it for, who's making the interesting work, what does it feel like, what does it change about being a person. The tech press writes a beat. Every writes the canon.

**What Every understands about software that media companies miss.** Software is a discipline, not a publication extension. Real customers, real bugs, real support, real money on the line every month. Media companies that bolt on software treat it as a marketing gesture. Every treats it as the actual work — the publication makes the software possible, and the software gives the publication new things to write about.

**What Every understands about culture that AI labs miss.** Capability without context is wasted. Labs ship models. Every translates models into something a person can pick up, play with, and use to make their own work better. The labs build the instrument. Every is the studio, the producer, and the magazine that tells you which records are worth listening to. AI is the new keyboard, the new typewriter, the new camera. Every teaches you how to play it well.

**What readers and users should feel Every gives them.** Taste, tools, clarity, status, leverage, companionship, access — in that order. Taste is what makes them subscribe. Tools are what make them stay. Clarity is what makes them recommend Every to a friend. Status is the byproduct.

**The master brand's cultural job.** Be a badge brand. Something covetable, a signal of intellectual curiosity and artistic grace. The place a curious, ambitious person goes to figure out what's worth caring about in AI — and to be handed instruments they can use to make better work. Not a wire service. Not a tool company. The thing in between, with a point of view sharp enough to cut. The studio that hands you the keys and points at the road.

**What would make Every feel like "just another AI newsletter."** Prompt-of-the-week roundups. Founder interviews with no opinion. Tool ranking listicles. "Top 10 AI agents to try this month." Manifestos with no specifics. Software that looks like every other AI product. A press release voice. A brand that could survive a logo swap.

## Campaign doctrine

A campaign works when it has a real strategic center. Not a theme, not a slogan, not a content calendar. A tension, enemy, belief, or behavioral truth that can generate many executions without going thin.

### Campaigns work when

Strong campaigns usually do these things:

- They make one idea famous.
- They dramatize instead of explain.
- They create memory structures: line, look, sound, ritual, character, format.
- They have a repeatable engine, not just one good asset.
- They resolve a tension the audience already feels.
- They fit the medium they live in.
- They make the brand easier to recognize after the campaign is gone.
- They give people language, status, or behavior to borrow.
- They compound over time instead of resetting every cycle.
- They create both immediate attention and future demand.

### Campaigns don't work when

Most campaigns fail because they are not campaigns. They are announcements, asset bundles, or internal strategy decks translated into public-facing copy. Bad campaigns usually come from:

- Feature lists pretending to be ideas.
- A vague "big idea" with no executional engine.
- A line that sounds good once but cannot generate a world.
- Trend-jacking with no brand transfer.
- Performance logic forced into brand media.
- Brand manifestos with no product behavior behind them.
- Creative that flatters the company instead of giving the audience something.
- "Awareness" work with no distinctive assets.
- Cleverness that requires explanation.
- Purpose language grafted onto a product that has no moral or cultural role.
- One-off stunts that create attention but no memory.
- Channel-native content that forgets the brand.

## Beliefs

Strong opinions, loosely held.

**We believe** marketing is making the brand more famous and more recognizable to the right people at the right altitude, repeatedly, over years. Building durable demand that the growth team converts.

**We don't believe** marketing is a performance channel, a lead-gen function, a content-calendar discipline, a graphic-design service, brand guidelines as a deliverable, or the org-chart slot under sales. Marketing at Every is distinctly tied to brand-building.

**We believe** creative works when it makes one idea famous, dramatizes instead of explains, builds memory structures, resolves a tension the audience already feels, gives the audience language or status to borrow, compounds across campaigns, and could not have come from any other brand.

**We don't believe** creative works when it's a feature list pretending to be an idea, requires explanation to land, flatters the company instead of serving the audience, is purpose grafted onto a product with no cultural role, is awareness work with no distinctive asset, or would survive a logo swap.

**We believe** brand is the cumulative deposit of every choice the company makes that the outside world can see — voice, look, products, prices, partners, posts, refusals. Brand is what the audience says about us when we're not in the room.

**We don't believe** brand is a logo, a color palette, a manifesto, or a tagline. Those are inputs. Brand is the output. But look and feel is extremely important.

**We believe** AI changes marketing by giving small teams with strong taste the ability to make work at a scale and bar that used to require a building full of people. The medium became playable. The constraint moved from production to judgment. Marketing becomes more like a recording studio than a factory.

**We don't believe** AI changes marketing by doing the same marketing faster, cheaper, or with more personalization tokens. That's the version every B2B SaaS company will run. It's not what Every does and it's not what makes the work matter.

**We believe** taste is the judgment that something belongs. Built through study, repetition, refusal, and willingness to be wrong in public. A muscle, not a vibe.

**We don't believe** taste is innate, mystical, or fixed. Not a synonym for elitism. Not preference. Not aesthetics alone. Not contrarianism for its own sake.

Brand is a promise. It creates a "smile in the mind."
