# Working With Douglas

Use these rules when developing, revising, or compounding substantial strategy, brand,
campaign, positioning, or executive work with Douglas Brundage.

## Start with the argument

- State the recommendation or tension early.
- Interview Douglas before drafting a long document when the brief is still ambiguous.
- Work one tab, section, or decision at a time when the document is strategically dense.
- Research current examples and channel mechanics before making recommendations that
  depend on them.

## Preserve the idea

Douglas supplies the problem selection, point of view, taste, and final quality bar.
Compression may remove repetition, throat-clearing, and low-value process detail. It
must preserve:

- The distinctive creative premise.
- The audience insight.
- The operating mechanism.
- The subscriber, Consulting, or authority path.
- The phased logic and decision gate.
- The cultural references, material details, or intellectual tension that make the idea
  generative.

If removing a detail makes the program sound like something any company could do, put it
back.

## Write actively

- Prefer "Every does" to "Every should do."
- Name the owner, audience, action, and result.
- Use direct, declarative sentences.
- Avoid passive voice, consultant abstraction, vague verbs, and excessive theory.
- Use theory only when it sharpens a decision, then translate it into action.

## Build two layers

Long strategies and executive summaries serve different purposes:

- The full strategy preserves the argument, creative system, evidence, mechanics, risks,
  and measurement.
- The executive summary supports a 10- to 15-minute review and presents the decision,
  rationale, priorities, measurement, ownership, asks, and budget.

The executive summary compresses the strategy. It never becomes the only surviving
version.

## Compound from edits

Douglas's edits are high-value evidence. When he returns an edited draft:

1. Compare the versions before drafting again.
2. Classify the changes: strategic correction, stronger point of view, specificity,
   voice, structure, evidence, ownership, measurement, or formatting.
3. Treat his version as canonical unless he asks for alternatives.
4. Update the relevant durable rule or project context.
5. Make the next edit locally. Do not overwrite the document with a new synthesized
   version.

The goal is to learn from the delta, not to reproduce the same mistake more fluently.
