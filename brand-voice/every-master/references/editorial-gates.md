# Every editorial gates

Run these gates on all Every parent-brand writing. They consolidate the Kate top-edit,
Every style, Every's AI-check, and the editorial tightening method.

## Pass 1: reporting and integrity

- Source quotations, data, research findings, rankings, performance claims, and factual
  assertions a reader could reasonably challenge.
- Set up quotations. Identify the speaker and explain why the quotation belongs.
- Identify people by full name and relevant role on first mention.
- Link to relevant Every archive coverage when it materially helps the reader.
- Separate observation, inference, and fact. Do not present an inference as reported fact.
- Verify product names, prices, dates, entitlements, and current organizational details.

## Pass 2: structure

- Find the thesis. If the piece has several, choose one and subordinate the rest.
- Put the signal, change, or useful consequence early.
- Cut whole paragraphs before tuning sentences.
- Remove repeated evidence followed by an unnecessary paragraph explaining the evidence.
- Remove mechanics paragraphs that narrate the writing or research process without helping
  the reader.
- Move vivid or useful details before cutting a weak section that contains them.
- End on the implication, decision, or action. Cut abstract “from X to Y” summaries.

For a structurally complete draft, test whether a cut of roughly 10 percent improves it.
This is a diagnostic, not a quota.

## Pass 3: sentences

- Put subject and verb early.
- Prefer active, specific verbs.
- Replace abstract labels with lived detail, a concrete example, or an observable behavior.
- Cut weak bridge sentences and connective tissue.
- Remove weak adverbs and stacked intensifiers.
- Simplify progressive and stacked `-ing` constructions.
- Preserve explanations that genuinely help the reader understand a technical term.
- Repair accidental fragments. Keep intentional fragments rare and legible.
- Replace vague `This` or `That` openers with the noun they refer to.

## Pass 4: AI-tell removal

Rewrite, do not merely delete, when meaning would be lost.

- Remove contrast templates: `not X but Y`, `Y, not X`, `not only`, `both`, `either`,
  `whether`, and cute binary pairs such as `signal, not noise`.
- Remove stock openers and transitions: `Here's the thing`, `Let's dive in`, `The key is`,
  `At its core`, `In today's world`, `That said`, and similar filler.
- Remove repeated clipped triads and strings of same-length declaratives.
- Limit em dashes. Follow Every house punctuation and prefer a period, comma, colon, or
  parentheses when cleaner.
- Remove sycophantic openings and generic praise.
- Remove clichés, motivational fog, and vague claims of importance.
- Challenge `real`, `really`, `actually`, `truly`, `essentially`, and `fundamentally`.
- Do not call complex work `simple`, `easy`, or `straightforward` without evidence.
- Do not swap one AI tell for another during revision.

## Pass 5: Every mechanics

- Use the Oxford comma.
- Use numerals for 10 and above unless house style requires otherwise.
- Keep capitalization, titles, product names, dates, times, and abbreviations consistent
  with Every's current style rules.
- Use sentence case where the relevant channel skill requires it.
- Keep punctuation inside quotation marks according to American usage.
- Avoid exclamation points except when the brief clearly earns one.
- Preserve intentional Every terms and product spellings.

## Pass 6: commercial and reader effectiveness

- Confirm the piece does its assigned job: inform, persuade, convert, orient, invite, or teach.
- Ensure the reader knows what changed, why it matters, and what to do next.
- Replace superlatives with proof.
- Remove fake scarcity, anxiety, and status pressure.
- Make each CTA concrete and consistent with the promise immediately before it.
- Confirm the tone fits the audience's relationship with Every.

## Final read

Read the draft aloud. Listen for repeated rhythm, hidden hedges, throat-clearing, and lines
that sound smoother than they are meaningful. Preserve the writer's specific taste and
experience. The goal is deliberate human writing, not sterile uniformity.
