# Thesis: 2027 Design and Brand Brief

**Status:** Working brief for creative development  
**Event:** Thesis: 2027  
**Date:** Nov. 5, 2026  
**Venue:** Pioneer Works, Red Hook, Brooklyn  
**Presented by:** Every  
**Strategic argument:** After Automation  
**Public descriptor:** The future of work. After automation.

## The Assignment

Create an identity for Thesis that can hold a conference, an editorial franchise, and an annual public argument about the future of work. It should feel like a credible civic institution built for the AI era: serious enough to carry consequential ideas, alive enough to earn cultural attention, and flexible enough to work on a phone screen, a printed token, or a room-scale projection.

The identity must remain recognizably connected to Every while giving Thesis its own name, mark, annual color, and visual behavior. Every should appear as an endorsement, usually through the Every logo. Use “An Every Conference” when an audience needs context.

## The Idea

**Thesis is the modern agora where humans frame what work becomes next.**

AI makes yesterday’s competence cheap. Productivity rises as machines perform more established tasks faster. Human value moves toward judgment: choosing the problem, setting the frame, making the big creative leap, and deciding what deserves to exist.

Thesis gathers people who have earned a point of view through work. Each speaker, writer, and attendee enters with a proposition. The conference and its editorial extension make those propositions public, testable, and useful.

The visual system should make contribution visible. An idea enters a shared field, takes a position, meets other ideas, and changes the frame around it.

## Positioning

Most AI conferences sell access to tools, operators, capital, or spectacle. Thesis treats AI and work as a civic and cultural question. It is a forum for people shaping the terms of the next economy.

The competitive gap:

- Enterprise AI events organize around adoption and efficiency.
- Creative-technology events often organize around novelty and atmosphere.
- Academic future-of-work forums organize around policy and ethics.
- Thesis organizes around human judgment, original propositions, and what comes after productivity becomes abundant.

Thesis should feel closer to a cultural institution, journal, and public assembly than a trade show.

## Audience

The primary audience is made up of ambitious professionals already using AI and thinking beyond tool adoption: founders, writers, researchers, investors, designers, artists, executives, and independent operators. They want a more precise account of what AI changes and a stronger frame for their own work.

The identity should reward intelligence without demanding design literacy. It should signal consequence without corporate futurism, cultural taste without fashion-event opacity, and optimism without utopian gloss.

## Brand Architecture

### Primary lockup

**THESIS: 2027**

The year is part of the primary mark. It makes the gathering feel like an annual volume and creates a clean mechanism for future editions.

### Secondary language

- The future of work. After automation.
- An Every Conference
- Presented by Every
- Nov. 5, 2026 · Pioneer Works · Brooklyn

### Every relationship

The Every logo acts as a maker’s mark or publisher’s imprint. It should not compete with the Thesis wordmark. Every’s master-brand typography and base colors provide continuity; Thesis gets a distinct wordmark, annual anchor color, editorial grammar, and image world.

## Art-Direction Thesis: The Civic Machine

“Civic Machine” names the identity’s behavior rather than an architectural style.

The system imagines public thought as something people build together. Contributions enter, occupy a position, combine, split, and reorganize the shared field. The visual language can borrow the logic of civic artifacts, tickets, ballots, seals, diagrams, publishing systems, and instruments. It should avoid literal temples, monumental portals, ornate chambers, and machine-age decoration.

The desired character:

- Civic, without bureaucracy
- Institutional, without austerity
- Cultural, without trend mimicry
- Mechanical, without gears or industrial cosplay
- Historical, without nostalgia
- Surreal, with a clear governing idea
- Orderly enough to use at scale, with controlled disruption

### Core metaphor: Pinakion and kleroterion

The Civic Machine is grounded in two specific artifacts from Athenian democracy.

The **pinakion** was a citizen's named identification token, made from bronze or wood. It made an individual legible inside a civic process. The **kleroterion** was the slotted allotment machine that received those tokens and organized citizens into public service through sortition.

For Thesis, the pinakion becomes the human token: a compact unit carrying a person's name, thesis, field, and edition. The kleroterion becomes the public field: the grid, sequence, and visible structure where individual positions meet. Together, they hold the brand's central tension. The person stays authored and distinct while entering a larger system of shared consequence.

The conference remains curated. Sortition informs motion and interaction rather than programming: tokens enter, rows release, positions shift, and the public arrangement changes.

This metaphor should govern the identity across badges, editorial bylines, session cards, the annual archive, environmental screens, motion, and the entry experience. It is the historical engine beneath the broader “Civic Machine” territory.

## The Anti-Deco Rule

The previous visual studies drifted toward Art Deco because symmetry, monumentality, gold-adjacent color, repeated vertical forms, and civic architecture appeared together. Remove those cues from the production system.

Avoid:

- Stepped or setback facades
- Fluting and repeated narrow vertical ornament
- Sunbursts, fans, crowns, and radiating geometry
- Arches, prosceniums, and monumental portals as recurring frames
- Black and metallic gold as a prestige code
- Zigzags, chevrons, and geometric luxury motifs
- Streamlined 1920s or 1930s display typography
- Beaux-Arts, World’s Fair, and grand-civic monumentality
- Gears, chains, pistons, and literal machine parts
- Marble busts, classical columns, and decorative heraldry
- Perfectly symmetrical compositions with ornamental centers

If a layout could advertise a luxury hotel, jazz bar, or 1930s ocean liner after a type change, it has gone too far.

## Visual Grammar

The identity should begin with four simple actions. These are compositional rules, motion rules, and editorial behaviors rather than literal icons.

### Contribution

A person places a proposition into public view. Represent this through a discrete authored unit: a token, line, block, phrase, image, or mark. The unit must carry information. Avoid blank circles, generic profile icons, and UI-toggle forms.

### Position

Each contribution occupies a specific place in a field. Use asymmetrical grids, indexed coordinates, numbered positions, margins, and purposeful adjacency. The system should feel arranged, not decorated.

### Assembly

Several contributions form a temporary whole. Blocks can align, overlap, interrupt, or reorganize one another. The resulting composition should make the relationship between ideas legible.

### Reframing

The field changes when a new proposition enters. A crop moves. A boundary opens. Type changes scale. An image reveals a different layer. This is the core motion principle and the clearest visual expression of the event’s argument.

## Core Identity Elements

### Wordmark

Design a custom `THESIS: 2027` wordmark. It should carry the project without an illustration and work at favicon-adjacent sizes, on a stage screen, on a lanyard, and in editorial headers.

The wordmark should feel authored, contemporary, and slightly strange. It may draw from inscriptions, marginal notation, printer’s marks, or typographic intervention, but it should avoid Deco geometry, faux antiquity, and tech-conference futurism. Explore the colon as a functional hinge between the enduring name and the changing annual edition.

Required versions:

- Primary horizontal lockup
- Compact lockup
- One-color lockup
- Small-size digital lockup
- Motion-ready construction
- Version with Every endorsement

### Human token

The human token is a supporting device, not the logo. Its reference point is the pinakion: a named civic ticket that gave a person a place in a public system. The Thesis version should feel like a contemporary cultural object carrying a name, proposition, or number.

It can appear as a badge, ticket, editorial byline unit, speaker identifier, or interactive object. Its shape should emerge from function and production. Avoid medallion ornament, profile silhouettes, coins with classical faces, or a generic person pictogram.

### Grid

Use a flexible editorial grid with visible positions, strong margins, and occasional reconfiguration. Default compositions should favor asymmetry. Formal symmetry is reserved for rare ceremonial moments and should contain a meaningful interruption.

Two layout modes are enough:

1. **Monument mode:** One proposition at large scale with expansive negative space. Use for the website hero, opening titles, stage statements, and key art.
2. **Assembly mode:** Several authored units arranged as a working field. Use for editorial indexes, schedules, speaker systems, social series, and wayfinding.

## Typography

Retain the Every master-brand type architecture.

- **Signifier:** Thesis statements, essay titles, keynote propositions, pull quotes, and high-consequence editorial language
- **Founders Grotesk:** Navigation, schedules, headings, labels, metadata, captions, signage, and interface copy
- **Every custom face or logo:** Master-brand endorsement only

The contrast between Signifier and Founders Grotesk already carries the productive tension: considered thought beside practical organization. Do not add a period display face to manufacture distinctiveness. The custom wordmark should provide that character.

Type should be used at decisive scale. Avoid ornamental letterspacing, stacked Deco title treatments, faux-engraved effects, and long centered passages. Use centered type only when the content earns a ceremonial pause.

## Color System

The permanent palette comes from the Every master brand. One anchor color changes each year so every edition can become a distinct volume without rebuilding the identity.

### 2027 anchor

| Role | Color | Hex | Use |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Annual anchor | Education Yellow | `#FFE667` | Wordmark field, key art, badges, hero moments, venue recognition |

### Usage

- Paper, cream, and black should carry most layouts.
- Yellow owns recognition for the 2027 edition. Give it space rather than scattering it as decoration.
- Blue, salmon, and mint should clarify structure, distinguish series, or mark a change in frame.
- Use flat inks and restrained print texture.
- Avoid gradients, neon-on-black palettes, chrome, metallic gold, and rainbow systems.
- Test every text and interface pairing for WCAG AA contrast. Yellow and pale accents will usually need black text.

The hex values above reflect the working Every master-brand palette. Confirm production values against the current Figma variables before final export.

## Image World

The image world should show thought becoming material. It can be surreal, but each image needs a clear relation to work, judgment, contribution, assembly, or reframing.

Useful source territories include:

- Civic tickets, ballots, indexes, annotations, and public records
- Instruments that sort, measure, register, or reveal
- Invented scholarly objects and diagrams
- Ordinary work objects placed in altered systems
- Bodies, hands, and faces interacting with propositions rather than posing as “the future”
- Impossible fields where language changes the space around it
- Contemporary reinterpretations of engraved, printed, embossed, stamped, and cut-paper processes

AI-generated imagery has a role in the entry sequence, environmental projections, editorial covers, and campaign films. Treat generation like a commissioned image practice. Build a controlled prompt and postproduction system, retain recurring visual rules, and art-direct every output. Raw model surrealism will quickly become generic.

Reject:

- Chrome blobs and glossy abstract 3D
- Nature-tech collage without a specific argument
- Astronauts, portals, floating heads, cosmic brains, and glowing orbs
- Marble statuary and classical portrait collage
- Anonymous hands touching holograms
- Pseudo-occult eyes, alchemical diagrams, and mystical geometry used as atmosphere
- Busy machine interiors, steampunk detail, and architectural fantasy
- Images that could belong to any AI conference after replacing the logo

## Photography

Photography should document people doing the work of the gathering: arguing, annotating, building, listening, testing, and exchanging ideas. Speaker headshots need a consistent treatment, but they should not drive the identity.

Use clean crops, a shared tonal curve, controlled color fields, and graphic interventions derived from the grid. Avoid classical frames, ornamental borders, faux archival degradation, and generic duotone as a substitute for an idea.

## Material and Texture

Thesis should feel printable and physical even on screen. Use paper tooth, ink gain, embossing, stamps, cuts, punched positions, and registration shifts with restraint. Texture should explain material or production, not age the work.

Favor humble civic and publishing materials: uncoated stock, enamel, powder-coated metal, cloth, rubber stamps, perforation, translucent vellum, and reusable modular signage. Avoid polished brass, black marble, velvet-rope luxury, and decorative foil as the main prestige signal.

## Motion

Motion should demonstrate reframing:

- A new proposition changes the surrounding grid.
- Type opens a field for an image.
- Units sort into temporary assemblies.
- A boundary moves to reveal a second reading.
- A contribution leaves a trace after it exits.

Keep movement direct and editorial. Avoid cinematic portal reveals, rotating gears, endless ambient morphing, floating particles, and interface motion that exists to look expensive.

## Verbal Behavior

Thesis inherits Every’s informed, human, playful, and specific voice. Its primary tone is Lucid with an Intimate secondary mode. Speakers and writers can sound like themselves; the framing language around them should be direct, precise, and generous.

The event should make claims rather than promise inspiration. Each major content unit begins with a proposition. Titles should read like positions a person could defend.

Editorial framing should answer:

- What does this person believe will change about work?
- What have they observed, built, or tested that earned the claim?
- What follows if the claim is true?
- What could disprove or complicate it?

## Editorial Extension: The Theses

The editorial franchise should begin before the event and continue after it. It turns the conference into a body of thought rather than a one-day content archive.

### Core format

Each commissioned contributor publishes one thesis about work after automation. A thesis should be specific enough to argue with and consequential enough to change a decision.

Recommended package:

- A one-sentence thesis
- A 1,000-2,000-word essay or a tightly edited interview
- The evidence or experience behind the claim
- One implication for the reader’s work
- One honest uncertainty or counterpressure
- A short contributor biography
- Audio reading when available
- Event relationship: speaker, participant, respondent, or commissioned writer

### Editorial design

Every thesis gets a numbered or indexed position in the annual field. The one-sentence proposition is the cover. Author imagery, diagrams, or commissioned art enter as evidence and texture rather than decoration.

The article template should include:

- Thesis wordmark and annual identifier
- Proposition at full scale
- Author and evidence metadata
- Reading time and format
- Shareable proposition card
- Footnotes, sources, and related theses
- A response or annotation mechanism if the product supports it
- Event CTA placed after the idea has earned attention

### Series behavior

Publish theses in a cadence that creates exchange. Pair pieces that disagree, commission short responses, and group the archive by question rather than industry. The archive should become a map of positions on work, judgment, productivity, creativity, management, ownership, education, and allocation.

## Surface System

### Website

The website must establish the argument before logistics. The first screen should contain the wordmark, descriptor, date, place, and one visual act of reframing. A user should understand the intellectual proposition within 10 seconds.

Recommended structure:

1. Hero: `THESIS: 2027` and “The future of work. After automation.”
2. The argument: a concise version of the After Automation frame
3. Featured theses: proposition-led editorial cards
4. Speakers and contributors: organized around their claims
5. Program: questions, sessions, and formats
6. The gathering: Pioneer Works, participation, and who the event is for
7. Tickets or application
8. Every endorsement and footer

The interface should use the same grid as editorial and venue materials. Avoid default tech-conference tropes: looping abstract hero video, logo clouds above the argument, animated gradients, speaker-card walls, and vague “where ideas collide” copy.

### Editorial hub

Create a persistent Thesis home on Every that can hold annual editions. It needs an annual index, topic map, contributor pages, essay pages, audio, event recordings, and cross-links into Every’s broader editorial work. The archive should remain useful after ticket sales close.

### Email

Email should behave like a dispatch from an active forum. Lead with a proposition, a new contributor, a revealed question, or a concrete program change. Use the yellow anchor for recognition, strong proposition typography, and minimal event chrome. Segment editorial readers, prospective attendees, confirmed attendees, and post-event viewers by job and timing.

### Social

Build recurring formats around authored claims:

- Proposition cards
- Short evidence excerpts
- Paired disagreements
- Annotated diagrams
- Speaker video with the thesis stated immediately
- Reframed quotes that show how a person’s view changed

Every post should carry the wordmark or annual identifier, a clear contributor credit, and enough context to stand alone. Avoid quote-card sameness and ornamental motion.

### Video and broadcast

Opening titles, speaker stings, lower thirds, holding screens, and recorded-session packaging should use the contribution-position-assembly-reframing grammar. The live broadcast must remain legible at small sizes and under compression. Reserve the surreal image world for openings, transitions, and commissioned interludes.

### Stage and screens

The stage should give ideas scale. Default to large propositions, generous fields, and useful metadata. Environmental screens can assemble multiple theses between sessions. Avoid building a literal civic facade or covering the stage in ornamental geometry.

### Entry and experiential projections

The entry sequence can carry the richest expression of the imagined Civic Machine. It should show ideas entering and changing a shared system through sound, language, scale, and responsive image. Build the experience from the same identity actions used on the website so it feels like the world behind the graphic system.

### Wayfinding and signage

Use position, numbering, color blocks, and direct language. Signs should resemble a well-designed public information system with moments of wit. Design for distance, low light, crowd movement, and Pioneer Works’ architecture. Decoration should never compete with navigation.

### Tickets, badges, and programs

Treat each object as a piece of the assembly. A badge can carry the attendee’s name and thesis; a ticket can assign a position; the program can operate as an index of propositions. These are places for the human-token idea to become useful. Personalization is optional and should follow privacy and production constraints.

### Slides and documents

Provide speaker templates that protect legibility without forcing every presentation into a house style. Thesis-owned slides should use proposition-first layouts, the two-type system, annual color, and the shared grid. Research reports, sponsor documents, and press materials should feel like parts of the same annual volume.

### Merchandise and apparel

Merch should carry a statement, useful object, or edition marker. Favor pieces people keep because the object is good: a field notebook, annotated poster, work jacket, cap, token, or reader. Avoid generic logo placement and pseudo-institutional crest merchandise.

### Sponsor system

Sponsors enter through a controlled endorsement layer. Define fixed logo zones, a limited palette, and rules for co-branded installations. Sponsor expression must not interrupt editorial independence or turn the shared visual field into a logo wall.

### Bring Your Agent experience

Any agent component should help attendees frame, annotate, retrieve, or respond to ideas. Give the agent a clear functional role inside the assembly. Avoid robot personification and “AI magic” language. Its visual state should derive from the same position and reframing behaviors used elsewhere.

### Press and partner materials

Press releases, media kits, partner cards, and share assets need the wordmark, descriptor, key facts, approved imagery, speaker claims, and Every endorsement. Create templates early so outside promotion does not flatten the identity into a generic event lockup.

## Annual Logic

The enduring system consists of the wordmark structure, Every endorsement, type architecture, visual actions, grid behavior, and editorial format. Each year receives:

- A new anchor color
- A new argument or organizing question
- A new body of theses
- One commissioned image practice or material expression
- A distinct edition number or year

The system should feel cumulative. By the third edition, the archive, printed matter, and physical tokens should read as volumes from the same institution.

## Creative Standards

Every proposal should pass these tests:

1. **Argument:** Can the choice be traced to contribution, position, assembly, or reframing?
2. **Distinctiveness:** Would it still be recognizable without the wordmark?
3. **Category freshness:** Does it feel new in AI events and future-of-work programming?
4. **System value:** Can the idea produce a website, article, sign, motion sequence, and physical object?
5. **Every fit:** Does it retain Every’s warmth, intelligence, print sensibility, and cultural curiosity?
6. **Usefulness:** Does it help someone read, navigate, understand, contribute, or remember?
7. **Restraint:** Has every historical or surreal reference earned its place?

If the concept depends on ornate architecture, period styling, or an image-generation prompt to feel distinctive, return to the system.

## Production Order

Develop the identity in this sequence:

1. Custom wordmark and endorsement lockups
2. Grid, type scale, color rules, and visual actions
3. Editorial thesis cover, article template, and index
4. Website hero, navigation, thesis cards, speaker system, and ticket flow
5. Key art and commissioned image-world tests
6. Motion grammar and broadcast package
7. Stage, entry, projection, and wayfinding system
8. Badges, programs, merchandise, sponsor kit, and press templates

Approve the core system after step three. Experiential work should prove that system at scale rather than compensate for an unresolved identity.
