# Concept Inflation: Thesis: 2027

Use this negative worked example when a memorable campaign, editorial, community, or experiential idea may be replacing the customer offer.

## What failed

An early Thesis: 2027 GTM treated “State your thesis” as the conference’s governing demand. That idea began as a useful website and editorial activation, but the plan propagated it into:

- The reason to attend
- Application questions and selection evidence
- Required post-acceptance preparation
- Public attendee profiles and matchmaking
- Program logic and event metaphors
- PR’s description of the event
- Primary success measures

The result was strategically coherent and wrong. Every attendee appeared to submit, publish, and defend a thesis. The actual paid value was strong panels about the future of work, intellectual clarity, and a curated network.

## Root cause

The planning process collapsed several roles into “participant”: ticket buyer, attendee, speaker, moderator, commissioned editorial contributor, and public website visitor. It also treated visible audience behavior as inherently better than watching, listening, learning, and meeting people.

Repetition made the invented premise look validated. Once thesis submission appeared in the offer, journey, PR brief, and scorecard, each section seemed to support the others. None traced back to an approved customer need or event requirement.

## Corrected value hierarchy

- **Reason to buy:** understand what AI is doing to work and meet people facing the same decisions
- **Core value:** strong panels, useful disagreement, practical clarity, and a deliberately composed room
- **Delivery:** credible speakers, sharp questions, rigorous moderation, Pioneer Works, and curated attendance
- **Strategic platform:** After Automation
- **Campaign and editorial support:** commissioned theses, reported pieces, speaker positions, and the optional public “State your thesis” prompt
- **Brand world:** Civic Machine, pinakion, kleroterion, and human-token devices
- **Non-requirements:** attendees do not submit, publish, or defend a thesis to apply, buy, or attend

The editorial activation still matters. It creates a runway, demonstrates intellectual range, and gives the public a way to participate. Its value does not depend on turning every attendee into a contributor.

## Transfer test

Run these checks on any GTM:

1. Remove the most distinctive activation. Does the offer still give the primary customer a strong reason to buy?
2. Does any application question judge a person on work the offer never promised to require?
3. Does onboarding assign homework mainly because the campaign needs content or proof?
4. Does PR describe an activation more prominently than the customer value?
5. Do primary metrics reward activation completion instead of purchase, use, learning, outcomes, retention, or return?
6. Have separate human roles inherited the same obligations without an explicit decision?

Two or more failures indicate concept inflation. Restore the upstream value lock, mark the activation optional, and rebuild the affected downstream sections.

## Correct behavior by product mode

### Consumption-led

The customer receives value through watching, listening, reading, using, learning, access, or connection. Required behavior should be limited to transaction, safety, logistics, and minimal service preparation.

### Participation-enhanced

Questions, profiles, introductions, polls, exercises, or public prompts may improve the experience. Keep them optional or lightweight unless the offer explicitly promises a participatory format.

### Co-created

Participant work is necessary to produce the value. Applications may assess contribution, onboarding may prepare it, and the scorecard may measure it. State that requirement before purchase and give it the proper consent, support, and recovery path.
