# GTM Plan

Builds the strategy that turns a launch narrative into demand, adoption, proof, and
learning. Use it for L/XL launches after the launch brief and positioning are sound and
before channel plans harden. The output should read like a sharp strategic choice, not a
calendar with a thesis added on top.

GTM sits at the point where Marketing and Growth meet. Marketing creates the belief and
memory structures. Growth converts that demand through the offer, first-use behavior,
activation, lifecycle, and measurement. Keep both mandates visible without collapsing
one into the other.

## When to invoke

- "Write the GTM plan," "build the launch strategy," or "how do we take this to market?"
- When a launch has positioning and a narrative but lacks a demand and adoption strategy
- When a draft is mostly dates, assets, or emotional value propositions
- When a launch needs a campaign idea, seeding plan, starter workflow, proof system,
  editorial role, video system, or measurable post-launch loop
- When reviewing whether a GTM plan is simple enough to guide decisions

## Required inputs

Do not invent missing product truths. Collect or mark OPEN:

- Confirmed launch date, product scope, pricing status, and buyer
- Primary audience, economic buyer, champion, daily user, and explicit non-target
- Customer evidence in their language: pain, current workaround, desired progress,
  adoption barriers, and the recurring work most worth changing
- Positioning: market frame, primary promise, functional proposition, emotional value,
  archetype, reasons to believe, and competitive distinction
- One verified internal proof point and the evidence bank for every external claim
- Business, activation, learning, and brand goals with owners

## Strategy standard

A complete GTM plan answers six questions in this order:

1. **What must the market believe?** State the category or behavior shift in one sentence.
2. **Who must believe it first?** Choose one primary customer and name the buyer, champion,
   user, and non-target. A list of adjacent personas is not a choice.
3. **What idea creates demand?** Define the campaign platform, enemy, demand, and brand
   payoff. These are four jobs, even when one line can carry more than one.
4. **What proves the claim?** Name the internal receipt, seeded-user evidence, customer
   receipts, and case studies that will make the strategy credible.
5. **What first behavior converts demand?** Choose one concrete starter workflow or job
   that creates meaningful value and can lead to repeated, shared use.
6. **What will we learn and measure?** Set dated business, activation, proof, and brand
   outcomes. Name the decisions those measures will inform.

If the plan cannot answer these questions on one page before expanding into execution,
the strategy is not ready.

## Campaign idea discipline

A brand line is a payoff, not automatically a campaign idea. A product instruction is an
activation step, not automatically a campaign idea. Build the campaign hierarchy:

- **Platform:** the identity, belief, or behavior the audience is invited to adopt
- **Enemy:** the costly default the audience recognizes in its own work
- **Demand:** the clear action or standard the campaign asks of the customer
- **Payoff:** the compact branded expression that Every can own over time

The idea must contain a human or organizational insight, generate many executions, and
make the product behavior feel like proof. Reject feature summaries, asset bundles, and
lines that only describe installation.

## Proof is the campaign engine

Build the launch around evidence, not posting volume.

- Start with one verified internal operating receipt and several real workflows.
- Show the request, context, action, approval, finished work, result, and what remains for
  next time. Do not call a product demo a receipt when no outcome is shown.
- Seed respected members of the target market before launch. Help each person complete a
  real recurring workflow more than once; do not script their conclusion.
- Continue seeding after launch. New use cases, credible advocates, and customer evidence
  sustain demand after announcement attention falls.
- Turn activated customers into quotes, artifacts, workflow receipts, referrals, and
  case studies. Each proof point should lower uncertainty for the next customer.

Set a publishable-evidence target. Ten credible receipts are more useful than 100 generic
mentions.

## Editorial's role

Use editorial to teach the market how to make the category work. For launches that
require new behavior, commission a definitive guide near launch. It should be genuinely
useful without the product, establish Every's method, and make the product the easiest
way to apply that method.

For an agent launch, the guide should cover choosing agent work, providing durable
context, defining a finished result, setting permissions, inspecting and correcting
work, turning repetition into skills, expanding to team use, and measuring value.

## Choosing the starter workflow

"Give it one real job" needs a named job behind it. Choose work that is:

- Recurring and important enough to repeat
- Dependent on context scattered across people or tools
- Easy for the customer to evaluate
- Safe enough to run with clear permissions and approvals
- Capable of producing a visible artifact or result
- Likely to involve a teammate, become a reusable skill, or run on a schedule

Avoid novelty demos, vague "help me run the company" prompts, and workflows whose value
cannot be observed within the activation window.

## Video system

Separate the brand film from the proof library. The brand film makes the campaign idea
memorable; the proof library makes the product credible and useful.

Define, at minimum:

- One branded launch film built around the campaign platform
- One 60–90 second product overview
- A high-volume library of short product interactions showing real requests, context,
  approvals, actions, artifacts, corrections, and retained learning
- Workflow stories that show a complete job and measurable result
- Customer and seeded-user receipts
- Creator kits, onboarding videos, website loops, and 6-, 15-, and 30-second cutdowns in
  the aspect ratios each channel needs

Every interaction clip should prove one claim. Produce enough examples to show range
without turning the campaign into a feature tour.

## Measurement

Lock four classes of measures with dates and owners:

- **Business:** qualified demand, paid conversion, revenue, retention, and referrals
- **Activation:** time to first value, successful starter workflow, repeat use, shared use,
  connected tools, skills created, and scheduled work
- **Proof:** publishable receipts, named advocates, case-study candidates, and referral
  stories
- **Brand:** awareness, branded search, direct and organic traffic, earned coverage,
  distinctive associations, and reuse of campaign language or assets

Do not report every available metric. Choose measures that decide positioning, onboarding,
pricing, channel investment, or the sustain story.

## Required output template

Use this section order. Adapt the depth to the launch tier, but do not replace the strategy
with a different consulting framework.

1. **Launch facts** — product, confirmed date, primary audience, buyer, champion, daily
   user, secondary audience, and explicit non-target.
2. **The tension** — the customer or market truth creating the opening, supported by
   verified evidence and the customer's current workaround.
3. **Campaign approach** — three–five numbered goals describing what the campaign must
   make happen in the market.
4. **Business and learning goals** — dated targets for demand, activation, conversion,
   proof, retention, revenue, brand, and the decisions those results will inform.
5. **Positioning for GTM** — framing, problem, audience, promise, brand line, functional
   proposition, emotional value, reasons to believe, archetype, and current competitive
   distinction.
6. **Campaign idea** — platform, enemy, demand, payoff, and a short articulation showing
   the human or organizational insight behind them.
7. **Dream outcomes** — one dream tweet and three–five sample dream headlines written
   verbatim. Each headline should validate the intended category belief or customer
   outcome; do not repeat the tagline with publication syntax around it.
8. **Proof and distribution** — internal receipt, real workflows, pre-launch seeding,
   post-launch seeding, customer receipts, case studies, permissions, and proof targets.
9. **Editorial** — the category-teaching asset, why it is useful without the product,
   publication timing, and supporting tools or worksheets.
10. **Activation** — the concrete starter workflow, first-use path, expansion into shared
    or repeated use, and the activation definition.
11. **Video system** — branded film, product overview, interaction library, workflow
    stories, customer receipts, creator kits, onboarding, cutdowns, and aspect ratios.
12. **Campaign shape** — pre-launch, launch, second use/activation moment, and sustain.
    Give each phase one job before listing its few essential assets.
13. **Measurement and reporting** — business, activation, proof, and brand measures with
    owners and dated review moments.
14. **Milestones** — only the real locks, publications, seeding starts, launch date, and
    reporting dates needed to run the plan.
15. **Open questions and claims** — unresolved product truths, pricing, permissions,
    unsupported claims, and the owner and deadline for each.

Write the first seven sections as the one-page strategic core when possible. Treat the
remaining sections as the execution appendix. Keep dates subordinate to strategic choices
and combine tasks that serve the same purpose. If removing a date changes the argument,
the draft is probably a project plan rather than a GTM strategy.

## Quick checklist

- [ ] The strategy fits on one page before the execution appendix
- [ ] One primary customer is chosen; buyer, champion, user, and non-target are explicit
- [ ] Campaign platform, enemy, demand, and payoff each have a clear job
- [ ] The big idea contains an insight and can generate a recognizable campaign world
- [ ] One internal receipt and a pre- and post-launch seeding system are defined
- [ ] One concrete starter workflow can produce visible value and lead to repeated use
- [ ] Editorial teaches the behavior the product requires
- [ ] Brand film and product-proof video library are scoped separately
- [ ] Business, activation, proof, and brand measures have dates and owners
- [ ] The required section order is preserved and includes one dream tweet plus three–five
      verbatim dream headlines
- [ ] Every external claim routes through claims clearance
